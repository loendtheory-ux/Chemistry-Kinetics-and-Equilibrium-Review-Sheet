Collision Theory & Potential Energy Diagrams

Two Requirements for a Reaction to Occur
① Proper Energy

Particles must collide with kinetic energy at least as large as the activation energy (Eₐ). Low-energy collisions are ineffective — particles just bounce apart unchanged.

② Proper Orientation

Reactive atoms must be facing each other at the moment of collision. The right energy but wrong angle = no reaction. Think: trying to shake hands with fists facing inward.

★ KEY
Activated Complex — the short-lived, unstable arrangement of atoms at the peak of a PE diagram. It can either break down into products (forward reaction) or re-form reactants (reverse reaction). It is not a stable molecule.
My Notes
Exothermic reaction — products lower than reactants
Activation Energy (Eₐ)
Activated Complex PE − Reactant PE

The energy "hill" to get over. Every reaction needs it — even exothermic ones. A catalyst lowers this barrier.

Heat of Reaction (ΔH)
Product PE − Reactant PE

Negative ΔH → Exothermic (releases heat)
Positive ΔH → Endothermic (absorbs heat)

Reverse Reaction Eₐ
Activated Complex PE − Product PE

The reverse reaction climbs up from the product level to the activated complex.

Exothermic Rxn
  • Products have less PE than reactants
  • ΔH is negative (−)
  • Heat is released — system warms surroundings
  • PE diagram ends lower than it starts
  • Ex: combustion, NaOH dissolving
Endothermic Rxn
  • Products have more PE than reactants
  • ΔH is positive (+)
  • Heat is absorbed — system cools surroundings
  • PE diagram ends higher than it starts
  • Ex: photosynthesis, NH₄Cl dissolving

Reaction Rates, Catalysts & Entropy

Factors That Affect Reaction Rate
Factor If you… Rate Why
Temperature Increase it ↑ faster More KE → more collisions exceed Eₐ
Concentration Increase it ↑ faster More particles → more collisions per second
Pressure (gas) Increase it ↑ faster Pushes molecules closer = higher effective concentration
Surface Area Increase it ↑ faster More reactant exposed = more contact between particles
Catalyst Add one ↑ faster Provides alternate pathway with lower Eₐ
📐 EX
Surface Area in action: 1 g of powdered zinc reacts faster with HCl than 1 g of solid zinc. Same mass — but powder has much more surface exposed. More exposed surface = more places for acid to attack.

How a Catalyst Works

A catalyst provides an alternate reaction pathway with a lower activation energy — like a shortcut through the energy hill.

Catalyst lowers Eₐ; ΔH stays the same
✅ Lowers Eₐ for both forward & reverse
✅ Speeds up both directions equally
❌ Does not change ΔH
❌ Does not shift equilibrium position
Heat of Solution (Ref Table H)
Exothermic dissolving (ΔH < 0) → solution warms
NaOH LiBr HCl
Endothermic dissolving (ΔH > 0) → solution cools
NH₄Cl NaCl KNO₃
Entropy & Spontaneous Change

Entropy (S) measures the disorder or randomness of a system.

Gas > Liquid > Solid  (entropy order)

Systems naturally move toward lower energy and more disorder. Exothermic reactions that increase entropy are the most thermodynamically favorable.

Chemical Equilibrium & Le Chatelier's Principle

What Is Chemical Equilibrium?
  • The reaction is reversible — both forward and reverse occur simultaneously
  • Rate of forward reaction equals rate of reverse reaction
  • Concentrations of reactants and products are constant — but not necessarily equal
  • System must be closed (sealed container)
  • It's dynamic — reactions keep happening, they just cancel each other out
  • On a graph: equilibrium = the moment lines flatten and stay flat
⚠ NOTE
Equal concentrations ≠ equilibrium. Constant concentrations = equilibrium. The two amounts can be very different from each other — what matters is that they stop changing.
My Notes
Le Chatelier's Principle

"When a stress is applied to a system at equilibrium, the equilibrium shifts to relieve that stress."

Stress Applied Equilibrium Shifts Because…
Add reactant → Right (forward) Uses up the added reactant
Remove reactant ← Left (reverse) Makes more reactant to compensate
Add product ← Left (reverse) Uses up the added product
Remove product → Right (forward) Makes more product to compensate
Increase temperature ← Away from heat Heat acts like a reactant/product — treat it that way!
Decrease temperature → Toward heat side Makes heat to compensate for the loss
Increase pressure (gas) → Fewer gas moles side Reduces # of gas molecules to lower pressure
Decrease pressure (gas) → More gas moles side Makes more gas molecules to raise pressure
Add a catalyst No shift Reaches equilibrium faster — same final position
🌡 TIP
Temperature trick: Find "heat" or "energy" in the equation. If it's a product (exothermic), heat is on the right — raising temp shifts LEFT. If it's a reactant (endothermic), heat is on the left — raising temp shifts RIGHT.
My Notes
Haber Process — Worked Example
N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) + heat

To produce more NH₃ (shift right):

  • ↑ pressure (4 gas mol → 2 gas mol)
  • Add more N₂ or H₂
  • Remove NH₃ as it forms
  • ↓ temperature (heat is a product)
  • Catalyst → faster equilibrium, same position
Phase & Solubility Equilibrium
H₂O(ℓ) ⇌ H₂O(g)

Requires a sealed container. Evaporation rate = condensation rate.

Reference Table G (Solubility):
  • On the curve = saturated = at equilibrium
  • Below the curve = unsaturated (can dissolve more)
  • Above the curve = supersaturated (unstable)
AgCl(s) ⇌ Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)
Add Cl⁻ → shifts left → Ag⁺ decreases, more AgCl(s) forms
